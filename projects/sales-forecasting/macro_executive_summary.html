The model explains ~66% of quantity variance — 34% remains unexplained

The best-performing model (Model C: ASP + Marketing + Seasonality) achieves R²=0.666, meaning approximately 34% of the month-to-month variation in quantity sold is not yet accounted for. Even including a lagged quantity variable (Model E), R² only reaches 0.689 — still leaving ~31% unexplained. This is a deliberate trade-off: the model prioritises interpretability and generalisability over overfitting to the training data. The sections below document the known sources of this unexplained variance and the planned steps to address them.

Known Limitations
1 · Data Quality — User-Forced Manual Entries

The transaction dataset contains rows where quantity = 0 but amount > 0 (349 rows identified across 72 months). These are ERP entries forced manually by users — likely to record service fees, promotional adjustments, or system corrections without a corresponding physical shipment. While the absolute value is negligible (27.5M VND total, <0.001% of revenue), they indicate the data is not a pure reflection of physical product movement and requires judgement in interpretation. Quantity-based metrics (units sold, ASP) are computed excluding these rows.

2 · Unexplained Variance — 34% of Quantity Change Not Captured

The current model (R²=0.666) leaves approximately 34% of month-to-month quantity variation unexplained. Known missing variables include: competitor pricing and promotions (not observable from internal data); distribution expansion (new customers, new regions — captured only indirectly via trend); product mix shift (changing sales composition within each month affects blended ASP and volume); trade promotion mechanics (volume discounts, free goods, end-of-quarter push — not separated from standard revenue); and weather and seasonality interactions beyond the simple sine/cosine approximation (e.g. heat waves driving beverage demand).

3 · Blended ASP — Masks Product Mix Effects

The current ASP is computed as total revenue ÷ total units across all products and channels. This means the ASP can rise not just because prices increased, but because the product mix shifted toward higher-value SKUs (e.g. more powdered infant formula 02XXXX, less condensed milk 01XXXX). True price elasticity measurement requires holding product mix constant — or decomposing ASP by product group. The elasticity estimate of −0.97 should be interpreted cautiously as it conflates genuine price effects with mix effects.

4 · Data Cap — Aug–Dec 2025 Understated

Five months in 2025 hit the 150,000-row Power BI export limit (Aug: 149,998 · Sep: 149,998 · Oct: 149,999 · Nov: 149,998 · Dec: 149,999). These months are truncated — actual transaction volume exceeds what was captured. As a result, revenue and quantity figures for Aug–Dec 2025 are likely understated, potentially distorting ASP calculations and model training for the most recent period. This also means the model's 2025 parameters are trained on incomplete data.

5 · Marketing Variable — Data Quality Error & Endogeneity CORRECTED

A material data quality issue was identified and corrected. The original marketing variable used SEGMENT3='20MK' from the 641 GL file (~1,095B/month), which captured the entire marketing department cost centre — including headcount (MH**), agency fees (MA**), and non-A&P lines. This created spurious correlation: larger business months naturally produce both higher sales volume and higher 641 department costs, regardless of actual advertising spend. Simple R²=18.5% (p=0.0002) was an artefact of this endogeneity, not a causal marketing-sales relationship.

The correct variable uses MK01–MK11 activity codes across all departments (~97B/month, 7,007B total). This genuine A&P spend is statistically insignificant at monthly aggregate level (R²=2.0%, p=0.23). Model C with correct spend barely improves on the baseline (R²=0.625→0.628, +0.3pp vs the spurious +3.7pp). Both models are documented. Marketing causal effect cannot be validated with current data — lagged spend or campaign-level attribution required.

The model explains ~66% of quantity variance — 34% remains unexplained

The best-performing model (Model C: ASP + Marketing + Seasonality) achieves R²=0.666, meaning approximately 34% of the month-to-month variation in quantity sold is not yet accounted for. Even including a lagged quantity variable (Model E), R² only reaches 0.689 — still leaving ~31% unexplained. This is a deliberate trade-off: the model prioritises interpretability and generalisability over overfitting to the training data. The sections below document the known sources of this unexplained variance and the planned steps to address them.

Known Limitations
1 · Data Quality — User-Forced Manual Entries

The transaction dataset contains rows where quantity = 0 but amount > 0 (349 rows identified across 72 months). These are ERP entries forced manually by users — likely to record service fees, promotional adjustments, or system corrections without a corresponding physical shipment. While the absolute value is negligible (27.5M VND total, <0.001% of revenue), they indicate the data is not a pure reflection of physical product movement and requires judgement in interpretation. Quantity-based metrics (units sold, ASP) are computed excluding these rows.

2 · Unexplained Variance — 34% of Quantity Change Not Captured

The current model (R²=0.666) leaves approximately 34% of month-to-month quantity variation unexplained. Known missing variables include: competitor pricing and promotions (not observable from internal data); distribution expansion (new customers, new regions — captured only indirectly via trend); product mix shift (changing sales composition within each month affects blended ASP and volume); trade promotion mechanics (volume discounts, free goods, end-of-quarter push — not separated from standard revenue); and weather and seasonality interactions beyond the simple sine/cosine approximation (e.g. heat waves driving beverage demand).

3 · Blended ASP — Masks Product Mix Effects

The current ASP is computed as total revenue ÷ total units across all products and channels. This means the ASP can rise not just because prices increased, but because the product mix shifted toward higher-value SKUs (e.g. more powdered infant formula 02XXXX, less condensed milk 01XXXX). True price elasticity measurement requires holding product mix constant — or decomposing ASP by product group. The elasticity estimate of −0.97 should be interpreted cautiously as it conflates genuine price effects with mix effects.

4 · Data Cap — Aug–Dec 2025 Understated

Five months in 2025 hit the 150,000-row Power BI export limit (Aug: 149,998 · Sep: 149,998 · Oct: 149,999 · Nov: 149,998 · Dec: 149,999). These months are truncated — actual transaction volume exceeds what was captured. As a result, revenue and quantity figures for Aug–Dec 2025 are likely understated, potentially distorting ASP calculations and model training for the most recent period. This also means the model's 2025 parameters are trained on incomplete data.

5 · Marketing Variable — Data Quality Error & Endogeneity CORRECTED

A material data quality issue was identified and corrected. The original marketing variable used SEGMENT3='20MK' from the 641 GL file (~1,095B/month), which captured the entire marketing department cost centre — including headcount (MH**), agency fees (MA**), and non-A&P lines. This created spurious correlation: larger business months naturally produce both higher sales volume and higher 641 department costs, regardless of actual advertising spend. Simple R²=18.5% (p=0.0002) was an artefact of this endogeneity, not a causal marketing-sales relationship.

The correct variable uses MK01–MK11 activity codes across all departments (~97B/month, 7,007B total). This genuine A&P spend is statistically insignificant at monthly aggregate level (R²=2.0%, p=0.23). Model C with correct spend barely improves on the baseline (R²=0.625→0.628, +0.3pp vs the spurious +3.7pp). Both models are documented. Marketing causal effect cannot be validated with current data — lagged spend or campaign-level attribution required.

Future Improvements
🔄 Next
Re-export Aug–Dec 2025 Full Data
Split Power BI export by region or sales channel to stay under the 150K row limit per file. Combine and retrain model on complete 2025 data. Expected impact: ~3–5% improvement in 2025 parameter estimates.
🔄 Next
Q1 2026 Out-of-Sample Validation
Upload Jan–Mar 2026 actuals. Compute MAPE per month vs model predictions. This is the true test of generalisation — model has never seen 2026 data. Target: MAPE <8% per month.
📋 Planned
Product-Group Level ASP & Elasticity
Decompose ASP and volume by product group (01/02/04/07). Estimate separate price elasticities per group — expected to show powdered milk (02) is more elastic than liquid milk (04). Enables more precise pricing strategy by category.
📋 Planned
Channel-Level Model
Separate regression models per distribution channel (GT, MT, KA, Horeca, Export). Channel mix shift is a major unexplained variance driver — modelling each channel independently would capture channel-specific seasonality, elasticity, and growth rates.
🔄 Priority
Lagged Marketing Spend + Correct Variable
The current MK** spend variable (~97B/month) is statistically insignificant at monthly aggregate level (R²=2%, p=0.23). Next steps: (1) Test 1–3 month lagged MK** spend to capture delayed ad response. (2) Investigate whether product-specific A&P (e.g. per-SKU promotional calendar) correlates with group-level volume. (3) Consider Nielsen/Kantar panel data as external marketing effectiveness benchmark.
📋 Planned
Trade Promotion Separation
Separate standard sales from volume-discount and promotional transactions (identifiable by discount code or price deviation from standard). Model baseline demand separately from promotional uplift — common practice in FMCG revenue management.
📋 Planned
ARIMA / Prophet Time Series Benchmark
Build a pure time-series model (ARIMA or Facebook Prophet) as a benchmark. Compare MAPE vs the current OLS regression. If ARIMA outperforms, it suggests the current features are not adding signal beyond what's already in the time series structure.
📋 Planned
Competitor & External Price Data
Incorporate competitor retail price indices (available from Nielsen/Kantar for FMCG) or proxy via relative price positioning. This would allow estimation of cross-price elasticity — how much competitor price changes affect own-brand volume — capturing the 34% unexplained variance.
